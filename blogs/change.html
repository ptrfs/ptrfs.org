The Science of Change

18 July 2024

Change, as a concept in the historical sense, can be described as the action of certain groups of people to alter the state of politics in a region due to a profound need championed by that group to enact policy. Change can be expected, as it often results from the culmination of multiple issues that eventually lead to a significant shift. For example, the Scottish National Party (SNP) in the United Kingdom wasn't a new, novel idea, but rather the culmination of multiple issues regarding Scotland and Scottish independence, which then coalesced into its own party.

Change can also be unexpected, which I categorize into two different types: Natural Unexpected Change and Unnatural Unexpected Change. Natural unexpected change is the kind that might happen, but we are never sure until it does. There's never a set date for when it will occur; we just know it will happen eventually. A couple of examples include Rishi Sunak calling the British General Election. We knew that Sunak would call a general election, but the timing was uncertain, with many estimating it would happen in August. When he called it in June, it took many by surprise, and his reasons remain somewhat of a mystery. Another example is the more recent development of Joe Biden's withdrawal from the 2024 Presidential Election. While it was likely to happen, no one knew exactly when, making it a significant event when it did occur.

Then there are truly unexpected events, such as the Cuban Missile Crisis. No one knew that the Soviets were placing nuclear warheads in Cuba, and when it was discovered, it nearly triggered a war.

Change is a constant in our lives, influencing everything from the advancement of science to new economic theories or businesses disrupting the current environment. It also extends to societal shifts, such as people accepting certain kinds of people. Change is primarily driven by the need of specific groups to alter the way they are governed. A common example is France, where the Ancien RÃ©gime's failure to represent a growing middle class led to the French Revolution. Change, therefore, is crucial because it can reshape the way people think for centuries to come. Gradual change, in particular, can have lasting impacts. For instance, after the fall of Nazi Germany, a poll revealed that some Germans preferred the previous authoritarian regime, not because they hated others or favored autocracy, but because the change they experienced before was perceived as better than the current one.

While change itself may not be historical, its methods can be. The rise of the printing press, for example, challenged the control of information by a literate few, leading to widespread critique of governments and eventual change. It allowed new ideas to spread globally, shaping the theories of development we know today. The Industrial Revolution transformed industry and manufacturing, prompting demands for governmental change, as seen with the Reform Act of 1832 in Britain and the Zollverein in Germany, which facilitated the unification of the country. The radio further accelerated the spread of ideas, enabling faster communication over short distances and fostering greater connectivity among people.

What will change look like now? It's difficult to say, but the proliferation of the internet has rapidly globalized communication and increased English language adoption worldwide. The internet combines the functions of the printing press and radio, leading to a steady invention and reinvention of political and economic theories. However, change can also fail. It fails when imposed against the will of the people and executed over a short time frame, as demonstrated by Hungary in 1956. A government installed by another, used to persecute its citizens, would never last, as people desire the freedom to make their own decisions. This also explains the failure of the French Revolutions before Napoleon; they were short, deliberate, and lacked popular support.

Change succeeds when it is implemented within the existing system, is familiar and popular with the majority, especially those in power. Successful change is realistic, goal-oriented, and accountable to the people who enacted it.

The best way to enact change, therefore, is through comprehensive, democratic, and far-reaching methods that allow people to become familiar with the change while still feeling that it is different. This approach is evident in many companies that continue to improve their products without completely reinventing the wheel. Reinvention can be too costly and risks losing customer interest, leaving only the most dedicated fans onboard. While I'm not suggesting that governments should be run like companies, deliberate and democratic change tends to have a longer-lasting impact.
